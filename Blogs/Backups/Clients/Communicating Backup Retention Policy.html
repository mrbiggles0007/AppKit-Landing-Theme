Importance of Backup Retention Policy

As a provider of documentation services to managed service providers in the United States, I have seen firsthand the importance of having a solid backup retention policy. A backup retention policy is a set of rules that determine how long backups of data should be kept before they are deleted. This policy is critical to ensure that companies have access to their data in the event of a disaster, and to protect them from data breaches and data loss. In this article, we will explore the fundamentals of developing an effective backup retention policy for managed service providers.

Defining Retention Policy

A retention policy is a set of rules that determines how long data should be kept, as well as how it should be managed and destroyed. When it comes to backup data, retention policies help ensure that data is available when it is needed and that it is disposed of properly when it is no longer needed.

Determining Retention Periods

Determining the retention periods for backups can be challenging, as it can vary depending on the type of data, the industry, and legal or regulatory requirements. Best practices suggest that backups should be kept for at least 30 days, and that backups containing sensitive data should be kept for longer periods of time. Ultimately, the length of time backups should be kept depends on the business needs of the organization.

Managing Backup Data

Once a retention policy has been established, it is important to manage the backup data. This includes performing regular backups, verifying the backups to ensure they are valid, and testing the restores to ensure that the data can be recovered in the event of a disaster. Managed Service Providers must ensure that their backups are encrypted to protect against data breaches, and that their backups are stored offsite to protect against disasters such as fires and floods.

Retention Policy and Compliance

It is important for Managed Service Providers to be aware of regulatory requirements and industry standards when developing their retention policies. For example, the healthcare industry has strict regulations under HIPAA, which requires healthcare providers to keep backups of patient data for six years. Failure to comply with regulatory requirements can result in significant fines and legal repercussions.

Documentation and Auditing

Documentation and auditing are critical components of a successful retention policy. Managed Service Providers should document their retention policy and regularly review it to ensure it is up to date and effective. Additionally, they should conduct regular audits to ensure that their backups are being managed and disposed of in accordance with the policy. Auditing helps Managed Service Providers identify areas where improvements can be made and ensure that they are complying with regulatory requirements.
