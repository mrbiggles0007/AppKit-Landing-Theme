Inheriting A Completely Undocumented Backup Environment 2023

Published 4 months ago5 min readBlogs About Cloud Backups HERE...

 

As a documentation services provider for managed service providers in North America, I have encountered numerous scenarios where the backup environment was completely undocumented. This can be a daunting challenge for any new IT team, and it requires a systematic approach to ensure that all critical data is protected and available when needed. In this article, I will share my first-person experience and knowledge on the fundamentals and basics of inheriting a completely undocumented backup environment. My aim is to provide useful insights and practical tips for IT teams who are facing this situation for the first time.

In the following sections, I will provide a step-by-step guide on how to assess the current backup environment, identify critical data, implement a backup plan, and monitor and maintain the backup environment. I will also discuss the importance of having a comprehensive MSP documentation system in place, which can help to streamline the transition process and ensure that critical information is captured and organized for easy access.

Whether you are a new IT team taking over an undocumented backup environment, or an MSP looking to improve your documentation processes, this article will provide valuable insights and practical tips. So, let's get started and explore the fundamentals and basics of inheriting a completely undocumented backup environment.

Assessing the Current Environment

When inheriting a completely undocumented backup environment, the first step is to assess the current situation. This involves identifying existing backup systems and software, understanding the current backup schedule, and determining backup storage locations. By doing so, you can gain a better understanding of the backup environment and begin to develop a plan for managing it going forward.

Identifying Existing Backup Systems and Software

The first step in assessing the current backup environment is to identify any existing backup systems and software that are currently in use. This may involve reviewing existing documentation, interviewing IT staff, or conducting a thorough inventory of hardware and software assets.

During this process, it is important to identify all backup systems and software that are currently in use, even if they are not being used to their full potential. This can help to ensure that no critical data is overlooked, and that all backup systems are properly integrated into the overall backup strategy.

Understanding the Current Backup Schedule

Once you have identified the backup systems and software in use, the next step is to understand the current backup schedule. This involves reviewing any existing documentation or interviewing IT staff to determine when backups are performed, how often they are performed, and what data is being backed up.

During this process, it is important to identify any gaps in the current backup schedule that may leave critical data unprotected. For example, if backups are only being performed once a week, there may be a risk of data loss if a failure occurs between backup intervals.

Determining Backup Storage Locations

The final step in assessing the current backup environment is to determine the backup storage locations. This involves identifying where backup data is being stored, how it is being stored, and how it is being protected.

During this process, it is important to ensure that backup data is being stored in a secure location that is protected from physical damage, theft, and other threats. It is also important to ensure that backup data is being stored in a format that can be easily accessed and restored in the event of a failure.

Identifying Critical Data

Once you have assessed the current backup environment, the next step is to identify critical data. This involves identifying data that is essential to business operations, understanding data retention policies, and determining backup frequency for critical data.

Identifying Data Critical to Business Operations

The first step in identifying critical data is to identify the data that is essential to business operations. This may include customer data, financial records, intellectual property, and other types of sensitive information.

During this process, it is important to involve stakeholders from different departments and business units to ensure that all critical data is identified. This can help to ensure that no critical data is overlooked, and that all stakeholders are aware of the importance of the data they are responsible for.

Understanding Data Retention Policies

Once critical data has been identified, the next step is to understand data retention policies. This involves reviewing any existing policies and procedures related to data retention, and determining how long critical data needs to be retained.

During this process, it is important to ensure that data retention policies are compliant with legal and regulatory requirements, and that they align with the organization's overall data management strategy. This can help to ensure that critical data is retained for the appropriate length of time, and that it is not unnecessarily retained beyond its useful life.

Determining Backup Frequency for Critical Data

When it comes to backing up critical data, determining the backup frequency is crucial. This is because you want to ensure that the data is protected and easily recoverable in case of any disasters.

Acronis

Acronis recommends backing up critical data on a daily basis. This is because daily backups ensure that you have the latest version of the data, and you won't lose any changes made during the day. Additionally, it's important to perform full backups periodically to ensure that you have a complete backup of your system.

Veeam

Veeam recommends using a combination of incremental and full backups for critical data. This approach ensures that you're only backing up the changes made since the last backup, which can save time and storage space. However, it's still important to perform full backups periodically to ensure that you have a complete backup of your system.

Datto

Datto recommends backing up critical data at least every 15 minutes. This ensures that you have the latest version of the data and minimizes the amount of data lost in case of a disaster. Additionally, Datto provides a feature called "Inverse Chain Technology," which ensures that you can easily recover data from any point in time without having to restore the entire backup.

Backup Exec

Backup Exec recommends backing up critical data on a daily basis. This ensures that you have the latest version of the data and minimizes the amount of data lost in case of a disaster. Additionally, Backup Exec provides a feature called "Granular Recovery Technology," which enables you to recover individual files and folders from a backup without having to restore the entire backup.

Carbonite

Carbonite recommends using a combination of full and incremental backups for critical data. This approach ensures that you're only backing up the changes made since the last backup, which can save time and storage space. Additionally, Carbonite provides a feature called "Hybrid Backup," which enables you to back up data to both the cloud and a local device for added redundancy and faster recovery times.

By following the backup frequency recommendations provided by these brands, you can ensure that your critical data is protected and easily recoverable in case of any disasters.

Best Practices for Documenting Backup Environment

As mentioned in the previous section, inheriting a completely undocumented backup environment can be overwhelming. However, with the right approach and best practices in place, you can ensure that the backup environment is well-documented for future management and maintenance.

Develop a Backup and Recovery Plan

The first step in documenting a backup environment is to develop a comprehensive backup and recovery plan. This plan should include information on the backup schedule, retention policies, and recovery procedures. It's important to document every aspect of the backup environment to ensure that there are no gaps in coverage.

Keep Documentation Up-to-Date

Once the backup and recovery plan has been developed, it's important to keep the documentation up-to-date. Any changes to the environment, including new systems, applications, or data sources, should be documented immediately. This ensures that the backup environment remains current and accurate, reducing the risk of data loss or corruption.

Use a Standardized Documentation Process

To ensure consistency and ease of use, it's important to use a standardized documentation process for the backup environment. This process should include clear instructions and templates for documenting backup and recovery procedures, as well as any changes or updates to the environment.

Include Relevant Information

When documenting the backup environment, it's important to include all relevant information, including system configurations, application dependencies, and any other details that may be useful for future management and maintenance. This information should be organized in a clear and concise manner to ensure that it can be easily accessed and understood.

Regularly Review and Update Documentation

Finally, it's important to regularly review and update the documentation for the backup environment. This includes reviewing the backup and recovery plan, ensuring that all changes have been properly documented, and making any necessary updates or revisions. By regularly reviewing and updating the documentation, you can ensure that the backup environment remains well-documented and up-to-date.
